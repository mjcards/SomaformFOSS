# SomaForm Analytical Framework

## Overview

SomaForm analyses swimming technique through the lens of applied biomechanics — specifically the principles of propulsion, drag reduction, timing, and coordination that determine efficiency in the water.

The framework draws on the foundational work of **Dr. Ernest W. Maglischo**, whose research and writing established the scientific basis for understanding competitive swimming technique. We attribute his contributions throughout this documentation and encourage direct engagement with his work.

---

## The End-of-Pool Assessment Model

SomaForm is designed around the **terminal wall zone** — the final 5 metres of approach, the turn, and the first 10 metres of push-off. This is:

- The most technically demanding phase of any length
- The phase with the greatest time savings available
- The phase most often neglected in training feedback

A fixed camera at the end of the lane captures the full sequence in a single, stable frame.

---

## Phase Breakdown

### 1. Approach (Final 5m)

**What we assess:**
- Stroke mechanics (catch angle, pull path, finish position)
- Breathing timing relative to stroke cycle
- Head position and its effect on body line
- Stroke rate and distance per stroke
- Any stroke count adjustment for wall approach

**Biomechanical basis:** Effective propulsion requires a high-elbow catch position that maximises the propulsive surface area of the hand and forearm. Body rotation (freestyle/backstroke) should be timed to the catch, not the recovery. (Maglischo, *Swimming Fastest*, Ch. 2–5)

---

### 2. Into the Wall

**What we assess:**
- Touch position (hand/feet relative to wall)
- Body alignment at contact
- Head position
- Stroke timing leading into touch

**Biomechanical basis:** Optimal touch position allows maximum force transfer on push-off without wasted rotation or repositioning time.

---

### 3. Turn Execution

#### Tumble Turn (Freestyle / Backstroke)
- Rotation speed and axis alignment
- Tuck compactness (moment of inertia minimisation)
- Foot placement on wall (width, height, angle)
- Time from touch to push

#### Open Turn (Breaststroke / Butterfly)
- Two-hand simultaneous touch
- Pull-through mechanics
- Foot plant speed and position

---

### 4. Push-Off

**What we assess:**
- Drive angle (horizontal vs. diagonal)
- Streamline position quality (head, arms, hips)
- Depth (optimal 0.4–0.6m for minimal drag)
- Duration before breakout kick begins

**Biomechanical basis:** The push-off is the fastest phase of any length. A tight streamline at optimal depth can sustain speeds 30–40% faster than surface swimming. Every degree of body misalignment increases drag quadratically. (Maglischo, *Swimming Fastest*, Ch. 9)

---

### 5. Breakout

**What we assess:**
- Underwater kick mechanics (amplitude, frequency, knee bend)
- Breakout timing (transition before velocity drops below swimming speed)
- First stroke mechanics
- Head position on first breath

---

## Scoring System

Each phase is scored 0–10:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Elite execution — no significant corrections |
| 7–8 | Competent — one clear priority correction |
| 5–6 | Developing — multiple corrections needed |
| 3–4 | Significant inefficiency — foundational work required |
| 0–2 | Safety or rule concern, or phase not detectable |

The **overall session score** is a weighted average, with push-off and breakout weighted most heavily (highest time-saving potential).

## Stroke-Specific Considerations

### Freestyle
Primary focus: high-elbow catch, hip-driven vs. shoulder-driven timing, bilateral breathing assessment, stroke counting on approach.

### Backstroke
Primary focus: shoulder roll, continuous kick, backstroke flags to turn count, touch timing (no flip required but legal touch matters).

### Breaststroke
Primary focus: two-hand simultaneous touch (DQ risk), pullout mechanics (single pull + kick underwater), breakout timing is especially critical.

### Butterfly
Primary focus: two-hand simultaneous touch, undulation vs. body dolphin, kick timing relative to arm pull, breathing frequency on approach.
